# Empowered Teams

**Source:** Marty Cagan (Partner at Silicon Valley Product Group, Author of "Inspired" and "Empowered")
**Best for:** Building autonomous product teams that own outcomes, not outputs

## Overview

Empowered Teams is a model for structuring product organizations where teams are given problems to solve rather than features to build. The fundamental shift: instead of leadership deciding what to build and teams executing, leadership sets outcomes and teams discover the best solutions.

This model comes from observing what separates the best product companies (Google, Amazon, Netflix, Apple) from mediocre ones. The best companies trust their teams to figure out how to achieve outcomes. Average companies treat teams as "feature factories" that execute roadmaps handed down from above.

The empowered model isn't just better for morale - it produces better products because the people closest to the technology and customers are making decisions, not executives several steps removed.

## How It Works

### Empowered vs. Feature Teams

| Feature Teams | Empowered Teams |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Given features to build | Given problems to solve |
| Measured by output (shipped) | Measured by outcomes (results) |
| Execute decisions made elsewhere | Make decisions themselves |
| PM as project manager | PM as product leader |
| Team as implementers | Team as problem solvers |
| Roadmaps are commitments | Roadmaps are predictions |

### The Empowered Structure

**Leadership's Job:**
- Set strategic context (vision, strategy, objectives)
- Define outcomes the team should pursue
- Provide constraints (time, resources, standards)
- Remove obstacles
- Coach and support

**Team's Job:**
- Discover the best solution to achieve the outcome
- Conduct customer research
- Design and build solutions
- Test and iterate
- Be accountable for results

### The Four Risks

Empowered teams are responsible for addressing four types of risk:

**1. Value Risk:** Will customers buy/use this?
- Customer interviews
- Demand testing
- Prototype feedback

**2. Usability Risk:** Can customers figure out how to use it?
- Usability testing
- Prototype testing
- Design iteration

**3. Feasibility Risk:** Can we build this with available resources?
- Technical spikes
- Architecture review
- Build vs. buy decisions

**4. Business Viability Risk:** Does this work for the business?
- Financial modeling
- Legal/compliance review
- Stakeholder alignment

### The Product Trio

At the core of empowered teams is the "product trio" - three roles that work closely together:

**Product Manager:** Owns value and viability risk. Understands customers, market, and business.

**Product Designer:** Owns usability risk. Designs experiences that serve user needs.

**Tech Lead:** Owns feasibility risk. Makes technical decisions and guides implementation.

These three work together daily, not in sequential handoffs.

## Building Empowered Teams

### Prerequisites

**1. Competent People**
Empowerment only works with skilled, senior people. Junior teams need more guidance. Hire for competence and give teams the talent they need.

**2. Genuine Trust**
Leadership must actually trust teams to find solutions. If you're going to override every decision, don't pretend you're empowered.

**3. Clear Outcomes**
Teams need clear objectives they're accountable for. "Build great products" is not an outcome. "Increase activation by 20%" is.

**4. Skill Development**
Empowered teams need training in discovery, customer research, and outcome thinking. Most PMs weren't trained for this.

### The Transition

Moving from feature teams to empowered teams takes time:

**Phase 1: Awareness**
Leadership understands the empowered model and commits to it.

**Phase 2: Coaching**
Bring in coaches or hire leaders who have experience with empowered teams.

**Phase 3: Pilot**
Try the model with one team. Let them struggle and learn.

**Phase 4: Expand**
Gradually expand the model based on success.

**Phase 5: Sustain**
Make empowerment the default operating model.

## When to Use

**Empowered teams work best when:**
- Building products that require innovation
- Customer needs are complex or changing
- Speed of iteration matters
- You have (or can hire) skilled product people
- Leadership is willing to trust teams

**Empowered teams are harder when:**
- Building to external specifications (agency work)
- Highly regulated environments with little flexibility
- Leadership isn't ready to give up control
- Team lacks skills for discovery

## Common Mistakes

### 1. Empowerment without accountability
Giving teams freedom without clear outcomes creates chaos. Empowerment means "you decide how" not "you decide if."

### 2. Calling feature teams "empowered"
Renaming your feature teams doesn't make them empowered. If teams don't have decision authority over solutions, they're not empowered.

### 3. Empowering the wrong people
Not everyone is ready for empowerment. Some PMs are skilled project managers but not product leaders. Build skills before expecting autonomy.

### 4. Leadership still making decisions
When executives override team decisions regularly, trust erodes and teams learn to wait for direction instead of acting.

### 5. Outcome avoidance
Some teams love the idea of autonomy but resist accountability for outcomes. Empowerment comes with responsibility for results.

### 6. Skipping discovery
Empowered teams must do discovery - customer research, prototyping, testing. If they skip this and just build what they think is right, they're not empowered, they're guessing.

### 7. Roadmaps as commitments
Empowered teams can't commit to specific features months in advance because they're discovering solutions. Roadmaps should be informed guesses, not contracts.

## Example Application

**Scenario:** E-commerce company transitioning to empowered teams

**Before (Feature Teams):**
- Leadership decides: "We need a wishlist feature"
- PM writes requirements
- Design creates mockups
- Engineering builds it
- No one measures if it helped

**After (Empowered Teams):**

*Leadership sets outcome:*
"We want to increase repeat purchase rate from 25% to 35% in the next quarter."

*Team explores the problem:*
- Interviews customers about why they don't come back
- Analyzes data on purchase patterns
- Maps the post-purchase journey

*Team discovers opportunities:*
- Customers forget about items they liked
- No reminder when items go on sale
- Post-purchase experience feels cold

*Team generates solutions:*
- Wishlist with price alerts
- Personalized re-engagement emails
- Post-purchase follow-up sequence

*Team tests assumptions:*
- Prototype wishlist with users
- A/B test email approaches
- Measure engagement signals

*Team decides:*
Based on testing, they decide to focus on price alerts and email sequence (higher impact than full wishlist).

*Result:*
Repeat purchase rate increases to 31%. Team learns what to try next.
