# Design Excellence Principles

**Source:** Katie Dill (Head of Design at Stripe, Former VP Design at Lyft and Airbnb)
**Best for:** Building craft and quality into products at every level

## Overview

Design Excellence Principles is a philosophy that treats craft and quality as non-negotiable features, not optional polish. The insight: beautiful, well-crafted products perform better on every dimension - trust, conversion, retention, and willingness to pay. Yet most companies treat design excellence as a "nice to have" that gets cut when time is tight.

Katie Dill's approach builds quality into the product development process from the beginning, not as a final polish phase. Design excellence isn't about making things pretty - it's about making things that work beautifully and feel right.

This framework is about raising the bar on what "done" means and maintaining that bar consistently across the entire product.

## How It Works

### The Four Levels of Design Quality

**Level 1: Functional**
The product works. It accomplishes the basic task.
*Minimum viable, but not competitive.*

**Level 2: Usable**
Users can figure out how to accomplish tasks without frustration.
*Expected baseline for professional products.*

**Level 3: Delightful**
Using the product feels good. There are moments of pleasant surprise.
*Differentiating quality.*

**Level 4: Beautiful**
Every detail has been considered. The product feels like a unified, crafted object.
*World-class quality.*

Most products stop at Level 2. The best products aim for Level 4 consistently.

### The Excellence Principles

**1. Craft at Every Layer**
Design excellence isn't just UI polish - it shows up in:
- Information architecture
- Interaction design
- Visual design
- Micro-interactions
- Error states
- Loading states
- Empty states
- Edge cases

*Test:* Pick any random state of your product. Is it designed, or just handled?

**2. Sweat the Details Users Won't Notice Consciously**
Unconscious quality affects perception. Users don't notice individual details, but they feel the cumulative effect.

*Examples:*
- Consistent spacing and alignment
- Smooth animations (no janky transitions)
- Thoughtful loading states
- Graceful error handling
- Considered empty states

*Result:* Users trust the product more, even without knowing why.

**3. Consistency Builds Trust**
Design systems and patterns create familiarity. When everything works predictably, users feel confident.

*Components of consistency:*
- Visual language (colors, typography, spacing)
- Interaction patterns (how things behave)
- Voice and tone (how the product communicates)
- Mental models (how things are organized)

**4. Polish is Not Optional**
The difference between good and great products is finishing touches. Great products feel complete.

*Polish checklist:*
- Are transitions smooth?
- Are states handled (loading, empty, error)?
- Are edge cases considered?
- Does everything align to a grid?
- Is spacing consistent?
- Are interactions responsive?

### The Excellence Process

**In Planning:**
- Budget time for design excellence explicitly
- Don't plan 100% capacity - leave room for craft

**In Design:**
- Design all states, not just happy paths
- Sweat the details before engineering starts
- Get design review from peers

**In Build:**
- Implement exactly what's designed
- Don't cut corners on animations or polish
- QA includes design fidelity, not just functionality

**In Review:**
- Review at full fidelity (real product, not mockups)
- Check consistency with existing patterns
- Ask: "Is this our best work?"

## When to Use

**Design excellence is always relevant, but particularly critical for:**
- Products where trust matters (financial, health, legal)
- Premium or B2C products
- Products in crowded markets where quality differentiates
- Brands that claim to value quality

**Signs your product needs more excellence:**
- Users describe it as "functional but not pretty"
- NPS comments mention "clunky" or "feels rough"
- Competitors are winning on perceived quality
- Team ships features but doesn't polish them
- Design debt has accumulated

## Common Mistakes

### 1. Excellence as a phase
"We'll polish it later" - but later never comes. Build excellence into the process, not as a final step.

### 2. Only designing happy paths
The edge cases, errors, and empty states get no design attention. These are often where quality breaks down.

### 3. Death by a thousand cuts
Each small quality sacrifice is "just this one time." Over years, these accumulate into a product that feels cheap.

### 4. Excellence only in new features
New features get attention; existing features rot. Excellence requires maintaining quality across the whole product.

### 5. Confusing polish with polish time
Excellence isn't about spending more time - it's about standards and craft being built into how you work.

### 6. Designers as the only quality owners
Everyone on the team (PM, engineering) should care about quality. Design excellence is a team value, not a design team job.

### 7. Tolerating inconsistency
"It's different because that's how the API works" - technical reasons don't excuse user-facing inconsistency. Find another way.

## Building an Excellence Culture

### For Leaders

**1. Model It**
Care about details yourself. Notice quality issues. Celebrate good craft.

**2. Protect It**
Don't let deadlines override quality bars. Push back when excellence is at risk.

**3. Resource It**
Hire great designers. Give them time. Invest in design systems.

**4. Review It**
Include design quality in reviews. Not just "does it work" but "is it excellent?"

### For Teams

**1. Define Your Bar**
What does "done" mean? Write it down. Make it explicit.

**2. Build a Design System**
Codify patterns so consistency is easy. Inconsistency is often accidental.

**3. Do Design Reviews**
Peers reviewing peers raises quality. Different eyes catch different issues.

**4. Track Quality Debt**
Keep a list of quality issues. Don't let them accumulate infinitely.

**5. Allocate Excellence Time**
Build polish sprints into your cadence. 10-20% of time on excellence.

## Example Application

**Scenario:** Implementing design excellence at a B2B SaaS company

**Step 1: Assessment**
Audit the product for excellence gaps:
- Empty states: 60% are just placeholder text
- Loading states: Most have no design
- Error states: Generic browser errors
- Animations: None (everything snaps)
- Consistency: 3 different button styles, 4 type scales

**Step 2: Standards**
Define excellence standards:
- All states designed (loading, empty, error)
- All transitions animated (200ms ease-out)
- All components from design system
- All copy reviewed for voice
- All features mobile-responsive

**Step 3: Process Change**
- Design review required before engineering
- Definition of done includes design QA
- Polish sprint (1 week) at end of each quarter
- Design system used for all new work

**Step 4: Investment**
- Hired a second designer
- Built component library
- Created animation guidelines
- Established design critique practice

**Result after 6 months:**
- NPS comments shift from "functional" to "polished"
- Customers mention quality as a differentiator
- Team takes pride in craft
- New features ship with consistent quality
